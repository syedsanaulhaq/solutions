# How to Write a Software Development Brief That Gets Accurate Quotes

## Why quotes vary so much

If you have ever sent the same project brief to three agencies and received quotes ranging from £8,000 to £80,000, you have experienced the vague-brief problem.

When developers cannot see the scope clearly, they either:
- **Pad the estimate** to cover uncertainty
- **Strip out** everything they assume is not included

Neither outcome serves you.

The brief you send determines the quality of the quotes you receive. A well-structured brief does not require technical expertise — it requires clarity about what you need to achieve and for whom.

---

## What every good brief includes

### 1. The problem you are solving

Start with the **problem**, not the solution.

- "We need a web app" — a solution
- "Our operations team spends three hours per week manually compiling reports from four different systems" — a problem

Developers who understand the problem can propose solutions that fit your actual need — sometimes simpler and cheaper than what you originally envisioned.

### 2. Who will use it

Describe your users specifically:

> "Three administrators who manage 200 learners across five companies" is more useful than "business professionals aged 35–55."

Include: how many users, how often they use the system, whether they are internal staff, external clients, or the public.

### 3. The key user flows

Describe the three to five most important things the system needs to do, as step-by-step flows:

> "Admin logs in → Creates a course → Assigns learners → Learner receives email and completes course → Admin sees completion in a report"

This is worth more than three pages of feature lists.

### 4. What the system connects to

List every system the new tool needs to read from or write to: your CRM, HR platform, payment provider, SSO system. Include whether APIs exist.

**Integration work is one of the largest cost variables in any software project.**

### 5. What success looks like

What specific, measurable outcome would make this project a success?

- Reduced manual work hours (by how many?)
- Improved completion rates (from X% to Y%?)
- Faster customer onboarding (from X days to Y days?)

Measurable outcomes let developers validate that their proposed solution actually solves your problem.

### 6. What you do not need

Be explicit about **out-of-scope** items. This prevents developers from including features you did not want and pricing accordingly.

### 7. Timeline constraints

If you have a real deadline, explain why. "We have a product launch in Q2" affects how a project should be staffed. If you have no real deadline, say so — it often reduces cost.

---

## What you do NOT need in your brief

You do **not** need to specify:
- The technology stack (unless you have genuine technical constraints)
- Wireframes (though they help if you have them)
- Database structure or API design

The technical decisions are what developers bring to the table. Your job is to describe the business problem, the users, and the constraints.

---

## Brief template

```
PROJECT BRIEF

Problem statement:
[What problem does this solve? Who experiences it? What does it cost today?]

Users:
[Who uses the system, how many, how often, internal/external?]

Core user flows (top 3-5):
1.
2.
3.

System integrations:
[List every external system: name, what data, direction, API available yes/no]

Success criteria:
[Specific, measurable outcomes]

Out of scope:
[Explicit list of things this project does NOT include]

Timeline constraints:
[Real deadlines and why, or "no hard deadline"]

Budget range (optional):
[A realistic range helps developers scope appropriately]
```
